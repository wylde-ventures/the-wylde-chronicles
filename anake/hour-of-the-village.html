Hour of The Village

February 16th 2015 Jeremiah
Non-time in the Southwest

The time of the village is 4:30 in the afternoon. This is the time that I’m really feeling the village. Feeling what a village feels like. It’s that time of the day where there is a pause between the hard work done out on the land during the meat of the day and the time that we all gather for dinner. There is the generally satisfied air of a day well spent that permeates the energy of the tribe. A willingness to lay down on the grass and really relax amongst each other.

Some are napping. Some are playing guitar. Some are lounging about in the hammock. Some are reading up on local plants. Some are chatting, or laughing, or singing, or crying. Some are stretching. Some are cooking.

Myself and a few others are sitting on a small and steep peak above this high desert valley in central california. Nestled against this peak in this valley below us is this village. Our tribe has come to visit this village and for a time merge our communities into one village.

The sun lies not far above the horizon. The light comes in almost horizontally. The shadows are long. The sky is crystal clear and it’s about 68 degrees. The sounds of the village at leisure are wafting up to this peak alongside the aromas of a stewing dinner.

The human sounds of laughter and guitar are mixed with the other residents of this village, the chickens, the goats, the ducks, the rabbits (not making much noise), and the herd dogs. Some of the native birds are chiming in as well. And behind it all, could that be the babble of a fresh spring-fed brook?

The sounds of the land, the sounds of the animals, and the sounds of the humans seem to come together in a fragrant kind of harmony. It’s borderline magical.

It’s curious to me that again and again during our stay here it’s been this time of day that the village has really come alive for me. There seem other more “obvious” times when we are “doing village” but that’s not when it clicks for me. Instead it clicks for me now, when we’re not “doing” anything. It’s in that liminal space that it snaps into focus.

The sun is beginning dip itself behind the mountain that stands a bit south of west. From the village below a quiet single voice begins to sing a different kind of song. It has no english words but is sung in the direction of the setting sun. One by one, the disparate voices and chatter and laughter from below start joining with this voice in this song. It becomes a sort of lullaby for the sun, a way of recognizing the warmth and light brought to us this day, tucking the sun in and sending him on his way. On the other side of the world, another tribe, in another village, sings another song as the sun rises.

The sun has faded. It’s now about 54 degrees and the dinner bell is ringing. The magical in-between time is over. With our hearts now full it’s time to trot off down the ridge and fill our bellies.
